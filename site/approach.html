Our Approach

How MCW shows up under collaborative contracts.

A practical view of what we bring to the Big Room, the design phase, and construction — written for owners and architects evaluating MCW for a collaborative-delivery pursuit.

Starting position

Collaboration is a behaviour, not a contract clause.

The contract form — IPD, CMAR, P3, Progressive Design-Build — sets the rules for risk, reward, and accountability. The behaviour is what determines whether the project actually delivers value. MCW operates under all four collaborative agreement forms with that distinction in mind: we treat the agreement as the floor, not the ceiling.

What that looks like in practice: senior engineers in the Big Room with authority to commit, real-time model updates rather than a build-up to a milestone, and an explicit commitment to share information that affects target cost as soon as it becomes known.

Operating principles

  • Co-design with trades, not for them.
  • Decisions made at the right level — not escalated.
  • Disclose pressure on target cost early.
  • Treat lessons learned as design input, not retrospective reading.
Delivery models

Four collaborative delivery models we work in.

The right contract form depends on Owner risk tolerance, schedule pressure, and how much design completion is expected before construction commits. We are fluent in all four.

IPD

Integrated Project Delivery

Multi-party agreement (CCDC 30 in Canada) with shared risk, shared reward, and a target cost. All key parties — Owner, architect, GC, key trades, engineers — sign one contract. Highest collaboration ceiling, requires Owner discipline and right-sized projects.

CMAR

Construction Management at Risk

Construction Manager joins the team early under a separate contract, providing constructability input through design and converting to a Guaranteed Maximum Price at construction. Mid-collaboration, lower contractual complexity than IPD.

P3

Public-Private Partnership

Long-term contract bundling design, build, finance, and (often) operate-maintain. Used for major civic infrastructure where private capital and lifecycle accountability are required. We have served as M&E consultants on multiple Canadian P3 pursuits and bring substantial bridging-consultant experience for owners and proponents preparing or responding to P3 procurement documents.

PDB

Progressive Design-Build

Design-Builder contracted early in two phases — collaborative design first, then a price commitment for construction. Captures most of the IPD/CMAR upside with a simpler contract structure.

The mechanics

Six practices we treat as non-negotiable.

Big Room cadence

Regular co-located sessions for transparent decision-making. We staff with seniors who can commit on the spot — never with junior placeholders waiting for direction.

PIT teams

Project Implementation Teams (PITs) tackle scope, schedule, and budget questions in parallel as task-specific working groups. We participate where mechanical or electrical decisions sit on the critical path.

Target Value Design

Design to a target cost, not toward a budget. We bring lifecycle thinking — energy modelling, system right-sizing, and constructability — to keep the team out of value-engineering rework cycles.

BIM-first coordination

Models are the source of truth, kept current in near-real time. Clashes are resolved in the model, with the trade contractor in the room — not negotiated in the field.

Lean methods

Last Planner, pull planning, and structured A3 problem-solving. Used as tools, not theatre. The aim is fewer surprises, faster commitments, and a flatter handoff into construction.

ICL discipline

The Incentive Compensation Layer aligns incentives only if everyone respects the math. We hold our scope honestly under shared pool arrangements and flag risk to the pool early.

A note on tools

BIM, lean, and energy modelling are tools — not the point.

The point is a building that performs and a team that finishes the project on better terms than it started. We deploy BIM, energy modelling, lean planning, and target value design where they earn their keep, and we resist the impulse to add process for its own sake.

Every IPD project we have delivered has been better off because the team agreed early on what they were trying to optimise. The tools followed.
